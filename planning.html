Competition Entry — Clief Notes #2

How this was planned

The process behind "Are We Okay?" — from brief to build. Every decision, every data source, every trade-off.

01 — The Brief

What Marcus actually needed

Marcus is a financial planner in Denver. 12 years. 60 clients — mostly dual-income families aged 35–55. His problem isn't finding clients. It's the first 20 minutes of every intro call.

He spends that time on basic math every prospect already could have done: "How much do I need? Am I on track?" By the time he gets to actual planning, the call is half over.

The brief was simple: build something prospects fill in before the call, so they arrive knowing where they stand — and why they need to talk to Marcus.

Judging criteria (Artifact Sprint): Does it solve Marcus's problem? Does it sound like Marcus? Is it simple enough to finish in 4 days?
02 — Strategic Decisions

What to build and why

1
Not an app. A calculator with a voice. The calculation is 5 inputs and one formula. What makes it valuable isn't the math — it's how Marcus responds to the result. That's where the brief lives.
2
Three result states, not a number. Telling someone they have $287,000 means nothing. Telling them they're 68% of target — and what that means — gives them something to walk into a meeting with.
3
Marcus speaks in the result. Every outcome has a voice-matched closing message written from Marcus's perspective. Direct. Honest. Never catastrophising. Always with a path.
4
Real data only. All benchmarks are from Federal Reserve SCF 2022 — public domain, citable, real. No fabricated stats. The "you're not alone" section is real or it doesn't ship.
5
Single file. No build step. Deploy in minutes, not hours. The competition is 4 days. Every hour not debugging Webpack is an hour spent on copy and craft.
6
Warm, not clinical. Most financial tools look like bank software. Marcus's clients are real people. The tool should feel like sitting across from someone who's seen your situation before.
03 — The Maths

How the calculator works

Three rules from standard financial planning methodology:

Rule Formula Source
25x Target Retirement target = annual expenses × 25 FIRE / Bengen's 4% rule
7% Return Future value uses 7% average annual return S&P 500 historical avg (inflation-adjusted ~5%, nominal ~7%)
Expenses Floor Annual expenses = income − (savings × 12), min 50% of income Prevents unrealistic inputs skewing results
Why 25x and not a specific number? It's universally understood, it's conservative (assumes 4% withdrawal), and it gives Marcus a clear opening — "here's why your number might be different from this baseline."
04 — The Research

Where the benchmark data came from

The "You're not alone" section uses real peer data. Every number is sourced and citable.

Data Point Source Notes
Median savings by age Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances 2022 Median retirement account balance by age bracket. Public domain.
Denver peer count U.S. Census ACS 2022 Approximate count of Denver Metro households in each age bracket at similar income levels.
Age brackets 30, 35, 40, 45, 50, 55 SCF reports in 5-year bands. Nearest band used for each user's age.
Rule applied: No fabricated statistics. If a number couldn't be sourced from public government data, it didn't go in. This is Marcus's name on the tool — it needs to hold up.
05 — Result Design

Three states, three voices

The calculator produces one of three outcomes. Each has its own copy, its own colour logic, and its own Marcus voice.

Ahead — ≥ 105% of target On Track — 90–105% of target Behind — < 90% of target
✓
Ahead: Validate without flattering. Marcus confirms the math is good, then opens the door to optimisation — tax efficiency, estate planning, sequence-of-returns risk. These clients still need a planner.
~
On Track: Honest about the gap. "Close isn't done." Acknowledges the progress while pointing to the small levers — contribution rate, retirement age, expense modelling — that make close into set.
!
Behind: Direct, not dire. "This is fixable." Marcus speaks from experience — he's sat across from people in tighter spots. The goal is to get them on the phone, not to panic them off it.
06 — The Build

How it was built and deployed

Brief read + strategy locked
Read the competition post and Marcus brief. Identified core problem, judging criteria, and what "done" looks like.
Design system decided
Warm off-white palette. Inter. Navy accent. No dark mode — this is for Marcus's clients, not a developer tool. Premium but human.
Real data sourced
Federal Reserve SCF 2022 median balances. Census ACS Denver metro peer counts. All public domain, no estimates fabricated.

5 inputs → FV calculation → 3 result states. Path cards for each state. Marcus voice on every output. No framework, no build step.
Copy written in Marcus's voice
Hero, subhead, all three result closings. Written as a prospect-facing tool — CTA is a real call booking, not a demo. Voice: direct, honest, never catastrophising.
Deployed — GitHub → Vercel
Git repo initialised in site/. Pushed to GitHub (IZZAPIZZLE/marcus-financial). Connected to Vercel. Auto-deploys on every push to main.
Social proof — in progress
Sourcing a real story: someone who turned their retirement around with a financial planner. No fabricated testimonials. If it can't be verified, it doesn't ship.
Stack

Technical choices

→
HTML / CSS / Vanilla JS. No React, no Tailwind, no build step. The whole tool is one file. A judge can view-source and see exactly what was built.
→
CSS custom properties as design tokens. One :root block controls the entire visual system. Changing the accent colour takes one line.
→
Inter via Google Fonts. The typeface does most of the design work. Used across fintech, legal, and healthcare — professionally neutral but warm at the right weights.
→
Vercel static deploy. Auto-deploys from GitHub main. No server config. Free tier. Live in under 60 seconds from first push.
